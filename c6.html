Setting

The last setting from chapter 5 carries onto chapter 6, at Gatsby’s estate, where they are encountered by a reporter, and it is revealed that Jay Gatsby’s real, legal names is James Gatz. Once Gatsby’s real name is introduced, the setting goes back to Gatsby’s childhood and early adolescenthood, where he was born in North Dakota, attended a college in southern Minnesota (where he eventually drops out), and spend a summer working along Lake Superior, where he meets the wealthy Dan Cody. Thus, his path to wealth begins.

Characterization

Jay Gatsby (James Gatz)

  • Physical Appearance - Narrator: “When he came down the steps at last the tanned skin was drawn unusually tight on his face, and his eyes were bright and tired” (Fitzgerald 109).
  • Thoughts, feelings, actions - “I’m delighted to see you… I’m delighted that you dropped in” (Fitzgerald 101). “Certainly; I’d be delighted to have you” (Fitzgerald 102). Narrator: “Then he kissed her” (Fitzgerald 111).
  • Other characters’ thoughts, feelings, actions - Daisy: “That girl hadn’t been invited. They simply force their way in and he’s too polite to object” (Fitzgerald 108).

Tom Buchanan

  • Thoughts, feelings, actions - “I’d a little rather not be the polo player… I’d rather look at all these famous people in---in oblivion” (Fitzgerald 105). “I didn’t hear it. I imagined it. A lot of these newly rich people are just big bootleggers, you know” (Fitzgerald 107). “Who is this Gatsby anyhow?... Some big bootlegger” (Fitzgerald 107).

Daisy Buchanan

  • Thoughts, feelings, actions - Narrator: “Daisy and Gatsby danced” (Fitzgerald 105). (To Tom) “Go ahead… and if you want to take down any addresses here’s my little gold pen” (Fitzgerald 105). “I like her… I think she’s lovely” (Fitzgerald 107).
  • Other characters’ thoughts, feelings, actions - “You didn’t look so interested” (Fitzgerald 108). Narrator: “What was it up there in the song that seemed to be calling her back inside” (Fitzgerald 108). “She didn’t like it… She didn’t have a good time” (Fitzgerald 109).

Conflicts and Themes

This chapter offers the long sought-after, finalized revealment of Gatsby’s past, exploring his love for luxury and lavishness, as well as his dedication to success and all that it has to offer. This element from the past can also directly be connected to Gatsby’s current standing in the novel. Though the love shared between Gatsby and Daisy is not known at this point in the story by Tom Buchanan, he has a strong distaste for Daisy’s tendency to frequent Gatsby’s acquaintance. Furthermore, during the party, the combination of the overbearing environment and Tom’s watchful eye placed Daisy in a state great discomfort, which was apparent to Gatsby. This caused him to question the realization of his dream of love and happiness with her, effectively stirring internal conflict.
