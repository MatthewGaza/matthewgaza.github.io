Setting

The story is centered during the 1910s and 1920s, around the World War I era. This chapter’s setting focuses on the East and West Egg of Long Island, New York. Nick’s home is in the West Egg, which is a less appealing neighborhood compared to East Egg, where East Egg is more luxurious. Only fine-looking house on West Egg is Jay Gatsby’s house, which is next door. The author, Nick Carraway, visits the home of Daisy and Tom Buchanan in East Egg, which he describes as "cheerful red-and-white Georgian Colonial mansion, overlooking the bay" (6).

Theme

Nick Carraway

  • Nick is gentle, simple, realistic, thoughtful, and alert.
  • Thoughts, feelings, actions - "And as I walked on I was lonely no longer. I was a guide, a pathfinder, an original settler" (4). "Now he was a sturdy straw-haired man of thirty with a rather hard mouth and a supercilious manner" (7). "Her face was sad and lovely with bright things in it, bright eyes and a bright passionate mouth..."(9).
  • Other Characters’ thoughts, feelings, actions - Daisy: "I love to see you at my table, Nick. You remind me of a----of a rose, an absolute rose. Doesn’t he?" (14).

Jay Gatsby

  • At this point, he is known as a high-class, wealthy man living in the West Egg.
  • Other Characters’ thoughts, feelings, actions - Nick: "Or, rather, as I didn’t know Mr. Gatsby, it was a mansion, inhabited by a gentleman of that name" (5). Discussed among the table at the Buchanan’s house about his prestigiousness.

Tom Buchanan

  • He is seen as self-centered, arrogant, dishonest, and cynical.
  • Physical Appearance - Nick: "...you could see a great pack of muscle shifting when his shoulder moved under his thin coat. It was a body of enormous leverage---a cruel body" (7).
  • Thoughts, feelings, actions - "I’d be a God damned fool to live anywhere else" (10). "I hate that word hulking... even in kidding" (12). "Don’t believe everything you hear, Nick" (19).
  • Other Characters’ thoughts, feelings, actions - Nick: "Two shining arrogant eyes had established dominance over his face and gave him the appearance of always leaning aggressively forward" (7).

Daisy Buchanan

  • She is seen as jittery, talkative, verbally strong, affectionate.
  • Physical Appearance - Narrator: "They were both in white, and their dresses were rippling and fluttering as if they had just been blown back in after a short flight around the house" (8).
  • Thoughts, feelings, actions - "I’m p-paralyzed with happiness" (8). (With a frown) "Why candles?" (11). "That’s what I get for marrying a brute of a man, a great, big hulking physical specimen of a--------" (12).
  • Other Characters’ thoughts, feelings, actions - Nick: "You make me feel uncivilized, Daisy... Can’t you talk about crops or something?" (12).

Jordan Baker

  • She is first seen as quiet, then independent, playful.
  • Physical Appearance - Narrator: "She was a slender, small-breasted girl, with an erect carriage" (11). Narrator: "Gray sun-strained eyes" (11).
  • Thoughts, feelings, actions - "I’m stiff... I’ve been lying on that sofa for as long as I can remember" (10). "We ought to plan something" (11). "You ought to live in California-----" (13)
  • Other Characters’ thoughts, feelings, actions - Narrator: "I enjoyed looking at her" (11).

Conflicts and Themes

The geographic distinction between East Egg and West Egg marks a conflict of themes that is likely to dominate the remainder of the novel. East egg is portrayed as a place of refinement and culture, whereas its West Egg counterpart represents a lack of both; their inhabitants are reflective of this, and commentary detailings of them set the stage for future happenings that are to unfold in adherence to said themes. For example, Carraway’s narration of his experience with Daisy, Tom Buchanan, and Jordan introduces an aristocratic, ’superior’ pattern that contrasts the gothic behemoth that is the West Egg lifestyle. This implies that the characters respective to each side of the thematic parallel are bound to clash, regardless of whether or not they have yet to interconnect. The first chapter, as it should, plants the seeds of conflict and resolution that are to sprout as progress holds.
