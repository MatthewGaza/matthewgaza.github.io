Setting

The chapter begins with Nick at Gatsby's house, where he encounters an unfamiliar butler and is ensured that Gatsby is doing fine. Gatsby has also hired new servants. The setting then transitions to the Buchanan residency, where Gatsby, Nick, and Jordan are all present. Then, the five adults take a trip to the city, where the truth between Gatsby and Daisy is revealed. This includes a stop at Wilson's garage. On their way, Tom, Jordan, and Nick took Gatsby's car while Daisy and Gatsby took Tom's car. On their way back, they stop at Wilson's garage again, only to see that Myrtle was caught in a hit-and-run and was instantly killed.

Conflicts and Themes

This chapter surfaces the latent conflict that had been stirring between Gatsby and Tom since Gatsby’s reunion with Daisy. Tom’s confrontation leads to his direct questioning of Gatsby’s relations with Daisy, facilitating the rebuttal that Daisy’s emotional loyalty remained with Gatsby even after their blissful past. Throughout the quarrel, however, Daisy finds herself growing ever closer to Tom, creating an emotional crossroad that causes Gatsby’s dream to depart further from reality. It is becoming increasingly clear to Gatsby that his loving conquest is not a fruitful endeavor at this point in the story, but his deep love for Daisy keeps his persistence alive and his heart adrift. His determination is illustrated thoroughly when he surrenders all that makes him grand to become a pitiful and helpless watchman from afar through the window and bushes that separate his dream from existence. Just as Gatsby once awaited his dream’s arrival from across the dock with outstretched arms, he now awaits the arrival of his love’s actualization from the other side of the river -- the location that spawned forth all he desires -- in a striking parallel of themes.
